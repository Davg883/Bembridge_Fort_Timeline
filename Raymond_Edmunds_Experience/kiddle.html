Hinckley Roots

Born on 31 October 1909 in Hinckley, Leicestershire, Geoffrey Oliver Kiddle was a product of an unbroken line of civic duty. Like his father, grandfather, and great-grandfather before him, he worked in the local bank as a clerk — a life of quiet, scrupulous precision with numbers and ledgers.

He married Margaret Jennetta Cholerton at St Mary's Church in Hinckley on 15 April 1939. When the Second World War broke out in September 1939, Geoffrey — remembering his mother's grief at the losses from the Great War trenches — volunteered for the Royal Navy at the age of thirty, choosing the sea over the mud of the front.

Anti-Submarine Warfare

Serving in the Royal Navy from 1940 to 1945, Geoffrey trained at HMS Osprey — the Anti-Submarine Warfare School at the Isle of Portland, Dorset. His civilian precision as a bank clerk made him ideally suited to the exacting, analytical demands of submarine detection.

Following training, he served briefly at the HMS Curlew shore base in Northern Ireland before receiving his posting to Bembridge Fort. He was assigned a cottage within Bembridge village, where he lived with his wife and young children, Bob and Jeanetta — a rare comfort that kept the family together on the Island even as conflict raged across the Channel.

⚓

Geoffrey's background in financial ledgers and careful bookkeeping transitioned seamlessly into the discipline of submarine loop monitoring — both required the same quality: absolute attention to the smallest movement in the numbers.

The classified underwater HD detection loops laid across Spithead — the first line of warning

The Needle of Truth

Geoffrey's most critical duty at Bembridge Fort was operating the Admiralty Pattern 599 Integrator — a classified instrument housed in one of the fort's cold, circular brick-vaulted casemates. The device detected the magnetic disturbance of a submarine's iron hull as it passed over the classified indicator loops laid on the seabed of Spithead.

When a U-boat crept beneath the surface into Spithead, the brass needle of the 599 would flicker. This sub-milliamp movement — invisible to the untrained eye — was the only sign that the enemy were there. The operators called it, simply, the Needle of Truth.

🧭

The Admirtalty Pattern 599 Integrator worked on a Mycalex ceramic base with brass precision fittings. Geoffrey would sit alone in the half-darkness, watching the needle for the faint twitch that could save Portsmouth Harbour. The bank clerk had become the first line of defence for the Solent.

He worked alongside a small team of WRNS officers in the plotting room — the Women's Royal Naval Service providing the same cool, analytical precision under pressure. Together they translated the silent signals from the seabed into actionable intelligence for the fleet.

Life at The Fort

Geoffrey wrote of his time on the Isle of Wight in vivid detail. He described Bembridge Fort as a "huge brick-built fort sunk into a deep dry moat on the summit of Bembridge Down." From the ramparts, he could see the whole length of Spithead, with barrack rooms converted into dormitories and galleys deep below.

"Behind these rooms a tunnel formed a circle of the fort so that each room had two entrances... At intervals were steep cuttings for the storage of gunpowder. It really was most impressive."

Commanded by a Harley Street dentist "cast in the role of Henry V," the operational reality was run by CPO Bishop, a highly efficient engineer. Geoffrey fondly recalled the self-important but likeable bosun, Leading Seaman Pickard, whose colourful vocabulary provided much amusement among the 45 Royal Navy personnel stationed there at Christmas 1940.

Despite the absurdities of garrison life, Geoffrey's letters home carried an unmistakable warmth. He admired the Island deeply — both its natural beauty and the camaraderie of the men and women who served alongside him under the shadow of potential invasion.

Left: Christmas 1940 — 45 men and women keeping a watchful eye on the Solent. Right: The imposing Victorian brick walls Geoffrey called home.

Beirut and a Return to Peace

Later in the war, Geoffrey was posted to Beirut, continuing his Anti-Submarine Warfare work until the conflict came to a close in 1945 — watching for an invasion that never came. His letters home from the Mediterranean spoke of beautiful weather and a longing for his family and the quiet streets of Hinckley.

Returning to his home town, he went on to serve as Manager of the Hinckley Permanent Building Society from June 1959 until his retirement in October 1974. As his father and grandfather had before him, Geoffrey's financial leadership enriched the community for years. He passed away in November 2005, leaving behind a profound legacy of service — both in peace and in war.

🏅

Geoffrey wrote everything down. His letters and memoirs are a primary historical record of life inside Bembridge Fort — the personalities, the routines, the tension and the dark humour that kept 45 souls going through five years of war. He was the fort's chronicler as much as its sentinel.
