Anti-Submarine Warfare and the Watch for Invasion

Hinckley Roots

Born on 31 October 1909 in Hinckley, Leicestershire, Geoffrey Oliver Kiddle lived and worked in his hometown, continuing a family tradition. Like his father, grandfather, and great-grandfather before him, he worked in the local bank as a clerk.

He married Margaret Jennetta Cholerton at St Mary's Church in Hinckley on 15 April 1939. When the Second World War broke out in September 1939, Geoffrey, remembering his mother's distress at the losses from the Great War trenches, volunteered for the Royal Navy at the age of thirty to avoid being called up for the army.

Geoffrey Oliver Kiddle, maintaining a proud family legacy

Anti-Submarine Warfare

Serving in the Royal Navy from 1940 to 1945, Geoffrey initially trained at HMS Osprey, the Anti-Submarine Warfare School at the Isle of Portland, Dorset—a direct response to the growing threat of submarine warfare.

Following his training, he spent time at the HMS Curlew shore base in Northern Ireland before eventually being posted to the imposing Bembridge Fort. He was given a cottage within Bembridge village where he lived with his wife and young children, Bob and Jeanetta.

Scanning the waters amidst the threat of invasion

Life at The Fort

Geoffrey wrote of his time on the Isle of Wight in great detail. He described the Fort as a "huge brick-built fort sunk into a deep dry moat on the summit of Bembridge Down." From the ramparts, he could see the whole length of Spithead, with barrack rooms converted into dormitories and galleys deep below.

“Behind these rooms a tunnel formed a circle of the fort so that each room had two entrances... At intervals were steep cuttings for the storage of gunpowder. It really was most impressive.”

Commanded by a Harley Street dentist "cast in the role of Henry V," the operational reality was run by CPO Bishop, a highly efficient engineer. Geoffrey fondly noted the absurdly self-important but likeable bosun, Leading Seaman Pickard, whose foul-mouthed vocabulary provided much amusement among the 45 Royal Navy personnel stationed there at Christmas 1940.

The formidable brick walls of Bembridge Fort

Beirut and a Return to Peace

Later in the war, Geoffrey was posted to Beirut, continuing his Anti-Submarine Warfare work until the conflict came to a close in 1945, watching for an invasion that never came. He frequently wrote home, admiring the beautiful weather and landscape, yet always longing to return to his wife and daughter.

Returning to Hinckley, he went on to serve as Manager of the Hinckley Permanent Building Society from June 1959 until his retirement in October 1974. Following generations of Kiddles, his financial leadership enriched the community for years. Geoffrey passed away in November 2005, leaving behind a profound legacy of service—both in peace and in war.
