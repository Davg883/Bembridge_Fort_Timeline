Amateur Radio to Cambridge Physics

Born in Dudley, Worcestershire, Maurice Wilkes developed a fascination with electronics early in life. Introduced to amateur radio by his chemistry teacher, he quickly demonstrated a profound understanding of radio propagation.

He completed his PhD in physics at St John's College, Cambridge in 1936, focusing on the propagation of very long radio waves in the ionosphere. This expertise would soon prove vital as the shadow of war fell over Europe, setting the stage for his critical wartime contributions.

Maurice Wilkes operating early radio equipment, 1933

The Radar War & Operational Research

With the outbreak of World War II, Wilkes was called up for military service. His deep understanding of radio waves led him to the Telecommunications Research Establishment (TRE).

Here, he worked extensively on the development of radar—the very technology that safeguarded installations like Bembridge Fort against aerial attacks. As the Isle of Wight transformed into a fortified frontline defending the English Channel, the operational research and defensive scanning technologies pioneered by Wilkes and his colleagues became crucial in detecting the Luftwaffe and coordinating anti-aircraft responses.

This invisible shield of radar secured the airspace, allowing operations at secret sites like Bembridge Fort to continue under the looming threat of invasion.

From Radar to the Stored-Program Computer

The end of the war marked a dramatic shift. Appointed director of the Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory, Wilkes encountered John von Neumann's description of the EDVAC. Reading the document overnight, he instantly recognised the future of computing.

Utilising his relentless practicality—honed during the wartime development of robust radar systems—Wilkes led the creation of the EDSAC (Electronic Delay Storage Automatic Calculator). Operational by May 1949, it was the world's second practical stored-program computer, a monumental leap that paved the way for the modern digital age.

Sir Maurice Wilkes: Transitioning from defensive technologies to computing

A Lifetime of Innovation

Wilkes didn't stop at EDSAC. He went on to invent microprogramming, which vastly simplified the design of Central Processing Units by controlling them with miniaturised programmes stored in high-speed ROM.

His innovations in computing, time-sharing operating systems, and distributed processing completely reshaped technology. Knighted for his contributions and recognised as a profound innovator, Wilkes is remembered not just as a computer scientist, but as a critical mind who helped defend the nation when it mattered most.

“...the realisation came over me with full force that a good part of the remainder of my life was going to be spent in finding errors in my own programmes.”

— Sir Maurice Wilkes
