Bringing 'Blighty' to the Front Line

In the heart of the Isle of Wight, behind the blackout curtains and windows criss-crossed with protective tape, the war felt both distant and terrifyingly close. Amidst the whizz-bangs and shrapnel rain, Master Raymond Edmunds, just 10 years old, stood on makeshift stages to bring a piece of home to the troops.

Whether at the Newport Military Mess or the highly classified Bembridge Fort, Raymond and The Stardust Company delivered over 400 concerts during the war.

"Despite the gunfire and bombs falling, the show must go on."

A performance at the Newport military mess, 1941

Makeshift Stages & Searchlight Beams

There was no glamour in wartime entertainment. Stages were hastily built from carpet-covered packing cases. Lighting was provided by military searchlights cutting through the darkness of converted holiday camps packed with over 1,000 sailors.

Yet, when the music started, the room fell silent. Raymond performed solo musical numbers, tap-danced, and often sang duets, becoming a Pukka star in the eyes of the British Armed Forces.

Isle of Wight holiday camp converted base, illuminated by searchlights

Greasepaint and Sausages

During the day, Raymond was just a "Barton Bonehead"—a pupil at the local boys' school. But the remnants of his nightly secret life were hard to hide. Thick stage greasepaint often lingered on his face the next morning, as he slept through the afternoons in the headmaster's study.

Under strict orders, his performances were kept secret even from his parents. His only wartime perk? A cooked meal of sausage and mash in the officers' mess, where the young boy would secretly pocket sausages for his father's breakfast back in Newport.

A faint shadow of a searchlight beam crosses the classroom window

A Treasure from Number 10

In 1945, the war ended, and so did ENSA. Raymond considered a career on stage, even landing an audition for a West End review. But when a theatre bombing thwarted his debut, he chose the steady hum of a shipyard drafting office over the limelight.

His story as the youngest ENSA performer of WWII was kept secret for nearly 60 years. But in his possessions lay a treasured artifact: a formal letter of congratulation for his unwavering service, signed by Clementine Churchill herself.

In 2018, Raymond returned to the abandoned Bembridge Fort. 75 years later, the echoes of the Wonder-Child still resonate within its historic walls.
